Reflective Statement

ENC2135  ·  Spring 2026  ·  Final ePortfolio

Writing for a specific audience means more than just picking the right words. It means figuring out how your reader is going to receive the information you give them, what assumptions they are walking in with, and what it is actually going to take to change how they think or what they do. Before I took this course, I knew that idea existed. What I did not know was how far off I was when it came to actually doing it, especially when the writing had real stakes attached to it.

The skill I got the most out of developing this semester is what I would call audience-centered framing. It is the ability to build an argument that does not just say what you want to say, but says it in a way that works for the specific person reading it, given what they already believe and what it would take to get them to act. I can see this skill most clearly in the revision of my Open Letter to Company Executives on Financial Transparency, which is the project I chose to revise for this portfolio. Two in-class activities helped me get there: the Bumper Stickers as Genres Practice and the Incorporating Evidence Practice.

The Bumper Stickers activity hit at a point in the semester when I was still thinking of genre as something you just recognize, not something that actively determines what your argument can and cannot do. A bumper sticker is not just a short opinion. It has maybe three seconds of a driver's attention, it needs to be readable from a distance, and the person driving is making a public statement from a vehicle. Those conditions set real limits on what the format can hold as an argument. You cannot present evidence on a bumper sticker. You cannot qualify your point. You have to assert, and you have to assert in a way that lands the moment someone sees it. Working through this with classmates forced me to explain why certain choices worked in that format and others did not, not because the writing itself was bad, but because it was trying to do something the genre could not support.

The Incorporating Evidence Practice built on that by showing me how evidence stops working when it is not set up properly for the reader. Going through examples of badly integrated quotations with my group made it obvious that a quote sitting in the middle of a paragraph without any introduction is just noise. It does not help the argument because the reader has no context for it. Our group identified four specific failure patterns during the activity: a paraphrase with no attribution, a quotation with no lead-in, a grammatical mismatch between the sentence and the quoted material, and a paragraph that started or ended with a citation before the writer had explained what to take from it. All four of those problems, in some version, showed up in my open letter draft. The data I was citing was there, but it was not doing any real work because I had not framed it.

Project 2 was where both of those lessons got tested together. The open letter is a genre that does something unusual: it is addressed to one specific audience but written for a much wider public at the same time. Every sentence I wrote for executive leadership also had to make sense to a general reader with no financial background. That is a framing problem, and it is exactly the kind of problem I had started learning to notice through the bumper sticker exercise. Managing two audiences at once means every decision you make has to work on both levels simultaneously, which is harder than it sounds.

My first draft failed in ways I can now name clearly. The opening line was "I am writing because," which tells an executive who reads twenty letters a week exactly nothing. It signals that the writer has not figured out what the argument is yet. I revised it to open with the actual problem, stated in terms an executive board would immediately recognize as something that affects their responsibilities.

The second paragraph used the word "create" twice in back-to-back clauses. In professional writing at this level, that kind of repetition reads as careless. The revision untangled those two clauses so that the ethical case and the business case each get their own statement, and then the connection between them becomes clear rather than muddled.

The phrase "improve their blind spot" is a mixed metaphor. You reduce or eliminate a blind spot. You do not improve one. For a letter aimed at executive leadership, that kind of error chips away at credibility in a way that is hard to recover from. The revision fixes the phrasing and also does a better job of integrating the Edelman Trust Barometer data and the behavioral economics research. Both sources now come with context explaining why they matter to the specific decision the letter is asking executives to make, which is the correction I learned how to make from the Incorporating Evidence Practice.

The first draft closed without giving executives any reason to act. It described a process for improving transparency but never said what would happen if they did or did not follow through. A call to action with no stakes behind it is not actually a call to action. The revision closes by being specific: there is a competitive advantage for organizations that get ahead of this, and there is a real cost for the ones that wait until the pressure forces them.

The skill this course built in me is going to show up in situations that have nothing to do with academic writing. Any time I am trying to get a specific person to make a specific decision, I need to think about who that person is, what they already believe, and what they need to hear in order to act. That is true for a job application, for a presentation to a group that does not share my level of familiarity with the topic, for any communication where the gap between what I know and what someone else needs has to be closed deliberately. What this course gave me is not a checklist. It is a way of thinking about writing that starts with the reader and works backward to the argument.
