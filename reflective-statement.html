Reflective Statement

ENC2135  ·  Spring 2026  ·  Final ePortfolio

When you are writing for a specific audience, there is a lot more to it than just choosing the right words. You have to consider your reader's perception of the material you provide as well as his or her preconceived notions about your subject in order to determine how to change their view or action through your writing. Before taking this class, I had a sense that these ideas existed, but I did not realize how far off-base I was when it came to actually implementing them, particularly when the consequences of my writing would have real-world implications.

The skill that I gained from this semester is what I refer to as audience-centered framing, which is being able to develop a persuasive argument that will resonate with the person that you are addressing and persuade them to take action based on their beliefs and assumptions. One of the best examples of this is the revised version of the Open Letter to Company Executives about Financial Transparency, which will be included in my portfolio. Two of the in-class activities that helped me accomplish this were the Bumper Stickers as Genres Activity and Incorporating Evidence Activity.

When I did the bumper stickers activity with my classmates, I was still thinking about genre as an element that shows up without knowing how it impacts your argument, but rather as something that will help you determine whether or not your argument will be successful. For example, bumper stickers are not merely quick thoughts; what they are really limited to is three seconds of the driver's attention, being legible at a distance, and being a public statement made while driving. Each of these conditions puts a very clear limitation on what the format will allow you to present as a case; you cannot provide evidence, you cannot provide qualifications, you must make a statement and you must make your statement at the time your audience sees it. By working on this project with classmates, I had to justify and explain to classmates why some of the writing was successful, while others were not, not because the writing itself was bad, but rather because it was trying to do something that the genre simply will not allow for.

The Incorporating Evidence work was a continuation of examining how the reader can stop understanding evidence completely if they do not have enough information available to lead them into understanding an example. Seeing what happened when we looked at multiple examples of poorly integrated quotes with my classmates, they were able to easily see how a vague quote dropped randomly in the middle of a paragraph without even any background information about it is basically useless and does not contribute to building an argument because the reader is unclear as to why this citation is relevant. Working through examples with my group, we identified four different types of errors related to integrating sources: 1.) A paraphrase without any attribution to the source, 2.) A Quotation without any lead-in whatsoever, 3.) A grammatical error related to the structure of the quoted material and the structure of the sentence quoting the material, and 4.) A paragraph that was either beginning or ending with a citation that the writer was not able to adequately explain how the audience was supposed to interpret it. These four instances related to incorporating evidence were seen in my draft open letter where evidence I cited was included but it did not actually have any value to grow the argument because of the way it had been presented.

The second project put both of these lessons to the test. The genre of the open letter does something unique: it has a dual audience; one specific audience receives the letter, while at the same time a much wider readership receives it as well. Every sentence I wrote for the executive leadership had to be understandable by a general readership with no financial background. This is an example of a framing problem, and it is similar to the types of problems I started noticing during the bumper sticker exercise. In addition, managing the executive leadership and a general readership simultaneously means that every decision you make must work for both, which is difficult.

My first draft had many different issues that I can now articulate. For example, at the beginning I started off with "I am writing," which gives no information to a decision-maker who receives twenty letters a week. It also indicated that the writer had yet to figure out their argument. I, therefore, revised the line so that it began with a statement of the actual problem; this statement would have been immediately recognized as the problems facing leadership boards.

Using the same word, "create," twice consecutively in two clauses makes the writing appear haphazard. By separating these two clauses out from one another in the revised version, both the ethical case and business case have an independent statement, and the connection between them has been clarified, rather than misrepresented.

The phrase "improve their blind spot" is also a mixed metaphor. You either reduce or eliminate a blind spot; you do not improve one. When writing to executives, this type of error diminishes your credibility in such a way that it is extremely difficult to regain. The phrase was improved in the revised version and provides a better integration of the Edelman Trust Barometer data with the behavioral economics data. Both types of evidence were better connected to the specific decisions requested of the executives in the letter; this was an example of the Incorporating Evidence Practice, and how it increased my ability to correct the text.

In the original draft, there were no clear consequences to motivate executives regarding potential outcomes of following through or not following through with their goal of increased transparency. Thus, there was no incentive for executives to respond to the call to action presented. The revised draft described a method by which to achieve this goal and added there are competitive advantages for early adopters and real costs for late adopters.

The new skill I have developed as an outcome of the course is the ability to use that knowledge beyond academic writing settings. I now have a much more concrete thought process in determining what needs to be communicated depending on the addressee's current beliefs regarding my intended message (e.g., on-the-job application; presentation to an audience with varying levels of familiarity with subject matter; any situation where I need to bridge the knowledge gap between myself and my intended audience). This course has provided me with new ways of approaching my writing from a reader-centric perspective rather than just a checklist.
