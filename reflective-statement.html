Reflective Statement

ENG Writing  ·  Spring 2026  ·  Final ePortfolio

Writing for an audience requires understanding how people respond to information and how that information is presented. Genre conventions shape reader expectations before a single word is read, and tone, framing, and evidence choices determine everything that follows. Before this course, I understood these principles as abstract ideas encountered in reading. What I discovered through completing the major projects is that understanding a concept and applying it under real constraints are two entirely different tasks, and the distance between them is where most of my learning happened.

The skill I developed most substantially this semester is what I would describe as audience-centered framing: the ability to construct an argument that accounts not just for what I want to say, but for who the reader is, what they already believe, and what they will need to encounter before they are willing to change their behavior. This is the skill most visibly developed through the revision of my Open Letter to Company Executives on Financial Transparency, which I have selected as the revised major project for this portfolio. Two in-class activities in particular, the Bumper Stickers as Genres Practice and the Rhetorical Rationale Practice, were the points in the course where I began to understand this skill well enough to apply it in my own writing.

The Bumper Stickers as Genres Practice arrived at a moment in the semester when I was still thinking about genre as a category of description rather than a set of active constraints. A bumper sticker does not merely state an opinion. It operates within strict limits of space, visibility, and the social context of a moving vehicle. Those constraints determine what arguments the form can make and how those arguments must be phrased to register with a reader who has approximately three seconds of exposure. Working through that exercise with classmates forced me to articulate why certain phrasings worked within the genre and why others failed, not because they were poorly worded in some abstract sense, but because they misread what the format could carry. That shift in thinking, from asking whether a sentence sounds good to asking whether a sentence works within the specific demands of its form, changed how I approached every writing task that followed it.

The Rhetorical Rationale Practice built on that foundation by requiring me to account for decisions I had already made in a piece of writing. Explaining why I had made a specific choice for a specific audience, in terms that went beyond "it seemed right," was harder than I expected. The practice made visible how much of my earlier writing had been driven by instinct rather than analysis. Instinct is not always wrong, but it cannot be revised systematically. Once I could name the rhetorical choices I was making and explain the reasoning behind them, I could evaluate whether those choices were actually serving the audience I was writing for. That is the capacity the Rhetorical Rationale Practice gave me, and it is the capacity the open letter revision required most directly.

Project 2, the Multigenre Persuasive Campaign, was where both of those skills were tested under conditions that mattered. The open letter format I chose presented a challenge the bumper sticker exercise had prepared me to recognize: the genre serves two audiences at once. An open letter is formally addressed to a named recipient, in this case executive leadership, while being written for a broader public who will read it as an argument about corporate accountability. Every sentence had to work at both levels simultaneously. It had to speak in terms executives find credible and actionable, while remaining legible and compelling to a general reader without a financial background. That is a framing problem, and it is exactly the kind of problem the Bumper Stickers exercise trained me to identify.

My first draft failed this test in several specific ways I can now identify precisely. The opening sentence began with the phrase "I am writing because." That sentence communicates nothing to a reader who receives twenty letters a week. It signals that the writer has not yet decided what the argument is. The revision opens instead with the specific problem, stated in terms an executive immediately recognizes as relevant to their responsibilities: that the gap between regulatory compliance and genuine transparency has a measurable cost in stakeholder trust that no amount of earnings management can recover.

The second paragraph of my first draft used the word "create" twice in close proximity. In polished professional writing, that repetition signals carelessness and undermines the argument at exactly the point where it needs to be strongest, where the ethical case and the business case converge. Untangling that sentence in revision required me to articulate both cases separately and then show their relationship, which is a structural decision, not a stylistic one.

The phrase "improve their blind spot" was a mixed metaphor. Blind spots are reduced or eliminated. That error costs credibility, and in a letter addressed to executive leadership, credibility is the only currency that matters. The revision corrects this and integrates the Edelman Trust Barometer and behavioral economics research with clearer attribution, so the reader understands not just what the data shows but why it is relevant to the specific decision being requested of them.

The first draft also concluded without consequence. It described a process for implementing transparency but gave executives no reason to act. A call to action without identified stakes is not a call to action. The revision closes with specific outcomes: the competitive positioning advantage that organizations gain by leading on transparency, and the cost of rebuilding trust that organizations face when they do not act.

The skill of audience-centered framing that this revision demanded is not limited to academic writing or professional correspondence. I expect to use it in any situation where I am trying to move a specific person toward a specific decision under real constraints. That includes job applications written for hiring managers with defined criteria. It includes presentations to groups with different levels of familiarity with the subject. It includes any situation where the gap between what I know and what another person needs to hear in order to act must be closed through deliberate choices about form, evidence, and tone. What this course gave me is not a set of rules. It is a method for analyzing a rhetorical situation and making decisions that are accountable to the reader rather than comfortable for the writer.
