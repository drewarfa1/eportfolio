Artifact 1: In Class Bumper Stickers as Genres Practice

In-Class Discussion Activity  ·  March 9, 2026  ·  ENC2135

What this artifact is: A three-part in-class activity completed on March 9, 2026, in which students used Canva to design bumper stickers from three different rhetorical positions: as an FSU student expressing personal identity, as a bumper sticker business targeting young Florida drivers, and as a seller pitching directly to the instructor. The activity required students to apply genre conventions deliberately and adapt their message to each distinct audience and purpose.

The Prompt

Part One: You are an FSU student with a car. You want to create a bumper sticker that is unique to you and persuades people to direct their attention towards the sticker.

Part Two: You are a bumper sticker business. You want to create a bumper sticker that appeals to young drivers in Florida in order to sell the largest number of bumper stickers and make the most profit.

Part Three: You are trying to sell the instructor a bumper sticker for her car.

Why This Artifact

This was the first activity in the course that made me think about what a genre actually does, not just what it looks like. A bumper sticker is not just a short statement. It has about three seconds of someone's attention, it has to be readable from a distance, and the person behind the wheel is making a public statement every time they drive. Those are real constraints, and they determine what you can and cannot argue in that format. There is no room for evidence. There is no room for qualification. You assert something, and it either lands immediately or it does not.

What made this activity stick was having to do it three times with three completely different rhetorical situations. The design that worked for an FSU student trying to express something personal had nothing in common with what worked for a business trying to move units to young Florida drivers, and neither of those worked for pitching the instructor on a bumper sticker for her own car. Switching between those three positions in the span of one class period made it clear how much the audience and the purpose reshape what any given format can do. That is the same problem I ran into writing the open letter, where I was managing two different audiences in one document at the same time.
