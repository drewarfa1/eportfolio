Artifact 1: In Class Bumper Stickers as Genres Practice

In-Class Discussion Activity  ·  March 2026

What this artifact is: A discussion board contribution from the In Class Bumper Stickers as Genres Practice, completed with classmates during March 2026. The activity asked students to analyze bumper stickers as a genre with specific formal and rhetorical constraints, and to articulate why certain arguments succeed or fail within those constraints.

This artifact reflects the development of my understanding of genre as a set of active formal constraints rather than a passive category of description. Prior to this exercise, I thought of genre primarily as a label. The bumper sticker analysis forced me to think about what the format can and cannot carry as an argument, and why.

Why This Artifact

The Bumper Stickers as Genres Practice was the first activity in the course that required me to think about what a form does rather than what it contains. A bumper sticker operates under strict limits: it has approximately three seconds of a reader's attention, it must be legible at a distance, and it exists in a social context where the driver is making a public statement from a moving vehicle. Those constraints determine what arguments the format can carry. A bumper sticker cannot present evidence. It cannot qualify its claims. It can only assert, and it must assert in a way that lands immediately.

Working through this exercise with classmates changed how I approached the open letter revision. Once I understood that the genre you choose determines what your argument can do, I was able to ask the right question about the open letter: what does this format require, and am I meeting those requirements? The answer, for my first draft, was no. The Bumper Stickers exercise gave me the vocabulary to understand why.
