Artifact 1: In Class Bumper Stickers as Genres Practice

In-Class Discussion Activity  ·  March 9, 2026  ·  ENC2135

What this artifact is: A three-part in-class activity completed on March 9, 2026, in which students used Canva to design bumper stickers from three different rhetorical positions: as an FSU student expressing personal identity, as a bumper sticker business targeting young Florida drivers, and as a seller pitching directly to the instructor. The activity required students to apply genre conventions deliberately and adapt their message to each distinct audience and purpose.

The Prompt

Part One: You are an FSU student with a car. You want to create a bumper sticker that is unique to you and persuades people to direct their attention towards the sticker.

Part Two: You are a bumper sticker business. You want to create a bumper sticker that appeals to young drivers in Florida in order to sell the largest number of bumper stickers and make the most profit.

Part Three: You are trying to sell the instructor a bumper sticker for her car.

Why This Artifact

This assignment for the course got me thinking about the function of a genre, rather than just its physical properties. An example would be bumper stickers. Bumper stickers are not merely short statements, they provide approximately three seconds worth of someone's attention (if you're lucky), they need to be legible even from a distance, and there is a driver behind the vehicle making an assertion every time a vehicle is driven. These are all very real limitations placed upon you, which defines how and if you can make an argument in that medium. The format is not able to carry evidence; it is not able to contain qualifiers; it only contains the assertion that it has just made, and it is entirely possible that it will not be successful with its claim right away.

Undergoing the process of creating a document or a design for three different audiences with three different purposes was the key to really understanding how important the audience and purpose are in determining how a document or design will work in a given format. The overall message and content of the FSU student's design meant something completely distinct for the business and even more separate for the instructor. Each time switching between three distinct groups in the time allotted for the class provided a definitive understanding of how the readership and purpose of a document have a direct impact on how the format functions. I experienced the same difficulty in writing my open letter when I attempted to write for two separate groups simultaneously.
