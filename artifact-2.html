Artifact 2: Incorporating Evidence Practice

In-Class Group Activity  ·  February 11, 2026  ·  ENC2135

What this artifact is: A group in-class activity completed on February 11, 2026. Students were given four poorly integrated quotation examples, each named after a common citation mistake ("I Swear I Did Some Research," "Armadillo Roadkill," "Am I in the Right Movie," "Dating Spiderman"). Groups were asked to identify the specific problem in each example and rewrite it correctly. The response below is from Nate Bock, whose group Drew Arfa was a member of.

The Prompt

Each of these attempts to incorporate quotes are annoying. Identify in which way(s) they are annoying, then correct them.

Group Submission (Nate Bock's Group)

Error Type Original Problem Corrected Version
I Swear I Did Some Research
Missing attribution on paraphrase 		The Seattle Seahawks crushed the New England Patriots in Super Bowl LX. They finished the night with a score of 29-13, after the Seattle Seahawks demonstrated their defensive strengths. [URL only, no in-text citation] Fixed
Barrows explains that the Seattle Seahawks defeated the New England Patriots in Super Bowl LX with a final score of 29–13. Their defense played a major role in the win and kept the Patriots from gaining any real momentum throughout the game.

Why This Artifact

The value of this exercise was that it helped me to see something that I would certainly have missed when writing an early draft; just because you include a source in a paragraph does not mean that your sources are arguing on your behalf. Without a proper introduction or set up to the quote in the paragraph, it is not evidence; it is simply text. In order to correct these issues, we had to work through four specific examples of these situations and identify what had gone wrong with each one. The process of labelling those broken citations allowed the lesson to hit home much stronger than it would if I had just read about the errors in citation.

While editing the open letter, this principle came back to me directly. In my first draft, I included not only the Edelman Trust barometer but also research in behavioral economics. In this case, however, they were included as citations in the draft but did not provide any context for the reader about what the findings were related to the specific request there was for the letter. When I resubmitted my revision, I provided context around each source prior to it being used in the document and offered an explanation of what the author wanted the recipient to take away from that source. This principle was demonstrated in this activity and I would have been unable to determine the issues in my submission without this experience.
