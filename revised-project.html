Revised Project

Open Letter to Company Executives on Financial Transparency  ·  Project 2, ENC2135

The first example shows my original draft of the open letter I created for the second project and a revised version of the same open letter. With regard to my revision, I focused on four key issues with my first draft: (1) my opening sentence was an ineffective hook; (2) my repetitive word use created an impression of disorganization; (3) my mixed metaphor undermined the validity of an argument; and (4) my conclusion contained only a procedural explanation without providing corporate executives any compelling reason to follow it. My intent in presenting both drafts is to show the reader each stage of my revision process.

The purpose of an open letter is to communicate to a reader before he or she reads any part of the letter - it serves as notice that there is something inside this document worth reading. The format I envision for an open letter will use standard letter formatting guidelines: Times New Roman font in size 12, double-spaced, includes a type/address block of the recipient followed by the date at the top. Using Times New Roman gives the open letter an institutionalized serious appearance consistent with the tone I want to convey to business leaders. Double-spacing allows for line spacing between sentences to aid readability and conveys more weight and pace than if traditional compressed typography were utilized. Therefore, my proposed format meets the definition of an open letter to be somewhere between private and public and makes this definition evident through its presentation. These design choices form the basis of my draft below.

First Draft

To the Board and Executive Leadership,

I am writing this because the distance between compliance with accounting rules and real visibility in business is larger than how most businesses have publicly described it. Behaviour economics research indicates how the presentation of financial information influences customer and investor behaviour.

The visibility case has both an ethical and practical reason for existing. Stakeholders should expect accurate representations of financial results. However, having a clear way to represent financial results will create a larger strategic advantage to the business than just creating an obligation to do so ethically. The Edelman Trust Barometer shows a decline in corporate trust, therefore any businesses who seek to improve their blindspot disadvantage will see an increase in the loyalty of their brand in a manner "managing the earnings" cannot accomplish.

Reporting in a more reasonable manner will not require a full scale redo of your Finance department, but instead will require replacement of any highly technical language used to define liabilities with language that can be easily understood. Additionally, when referring to performance metrics such as "adjusted," clear explanations of the meaning should be provided. All of this centres on how you treat and communicate with the company's business stakeholders.

Revised Draft

To the Board and Executive Leadership,

Your company will not be able to regain trust from its stakeholders through just manipulation of your earnings report, but the gap between compliance and real transparency is causing you to lose that trust. Research done within behavior-based economics has shown that the way that financial reporting is communicated to customers and shareholders can have a huge impact on their ultimate decision-making, even if they do not understand the actual numbers.

This gap is much wider than most companies realize, and the financial impact of ignoring the gap is quantifiable.

The argument for transparency can be made on both ethical grounds and practical grounds. Stakeholders deserve accurate financial reporting. Moreover, by reporting financial results in a way that is easy to read and understand, these companies will have a structural advantage over other companies that do not report in that way. The Edelman Trust Barometer shows that there is a steady decline in trust in corporations in every industry. Those companies that are willing to remove all of the blind spots in their financial reporting can expect to see increases in customer loyalty that a company cannot produce through manipulation of the numbers only.

Behaviorally sound reporting can be achieved by making two targeted changes to the way your organization communicates financial information: replace technical liability terms with terms that can be understood by all stakeholders regardless of their financial background, and define all performance metrics with qualifiers explicitly. Your organization's choice regarding how it treats the individuals it serves is also reflected in the way it communicates with them.

Transparent organizations today will have a competitive edge over those that do not because of the tightening of regulator disclosure requirements and because of the growing influence of governance quality in investment allocation. Organizations that do not develop transparent practices will incur much greater costs to repair their lost trust than they would if they had maintained their trust. This letter requests that your corporate leadership take an action to beginning improving its financial communication by commissioning a readability audit of your current financial communications against plain-language readability standards. The return on this investment will be evident in your ability to retain stakeholders prior to your next reporting cycle.

What Changed and Why

First line opening: "I am writing because" tells a busy executive nothing useful. They will assume you are still trying to determine what you want to say based on the fact that you have not stated a clear issue. By starting with the actual problem as defined in terms relevant to the executive board members will help them understand their own responsibilities.

Repetitive language. The word "create" appears twice within two consecutive clauses thereby indicating to anyone who has been watching this letter is poorly written due to the inconsistency of vocabulary use. The revision separates the ethical and the business cases so each has its own statement of their respective purposes and allows for the correlation between the two cases to become apparent.

The mixed metaphor means you cannot improve (1) a blind spot because you can either reduce (2) or eliminate (3) it. When you use the word "improve," there is a problem with the logical flow of your letter and could affect the credibility of the content to executive leadership more than in any other letter. The rewrite corrects this language error and maintains the intent.

There are no "consequences" at the conclusion of the original letter because you talked about the process for putting changes into place. The revision also provides the "pros" of doing the changes and the "cons" of waiting until the changes occur as a result of external forces.
