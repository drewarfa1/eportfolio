Revised Project

Open Letter to Company Executives on Financial Transparency  ·  Project 2, ENC2135

Below are my first draft and revised draft of the open letter from Project 2. I focused my revision on four problems in the original: (1) my opening sentence was a weak hook; (2) repetitive word use made the argument look disorganized; (3) a mixed metaphor undermined the argument's credibility; and (4) the conclusion gave executives no reason to act. Both drafts are included so the revision process is visible.

An open letter announces its purpose before the reader gets to the first line. The format I chose follows standard formal letter conventions: Times New Roman, 12-point, double-spaced, with a recipient address block and date at the top. Times New Roman reads as institutional and serious, which suits the register of writing to corporate leadership. Double spacing gives each line weight and pace that compressed text cannot. The genre falls between private correspondence and public statement, and the format makes that positioning clear.

First Draft

To the Board and Executive Leadership,

I am writing because research in behavioral economics shows that how financial information is presented influences both customers and investors in their decisions.

The case for visibility is both ethical and practical. Stakeholders deserve accurate representations of financial performance; however, the motivation to provide clearly expressed financial reports creates a greater opportunity to create long-term competitive advantage than the ethical obligation alone. The Edelman Trust Barometer shows a decline in corporate trust, and businesses willing to improve their blind spot around financial visibility will see higher brand loyalty than companies reliant on earnings management alone.

Reporting in a more behaviorally sound way does not require restructuring your Finance team. It requires replacing the technical language you use to describe liabilities with terminology your stakeholders can follow, and explaining what "adjusted" means when used to qualify performance metrics. This is about how you treat the stakeholders your company serves.

Revised Draft

To the Board and Executive Leadership,

Your company is losing stakeholder trust that earnings manipulation alone cannot recover. Behavioral economics research shows that how financial reporting is communicated to customers and shareholders shapes their decisions, regardless of what the numbers say. That gap between compliance and real transparency is wider than most companies admit, and the cost is measurable.

Transparency has both an ethical and a practical case. Stakeholders deserve accurate financial reporting. Companies that present results in plain terms hold a structural advantage over those that do not. The Edelman Trust Barometer documents a steady decline in corporate trust across every industry. Companies willing to remove the blind spots in their financial reporting will see customer loyalty increases that earnings management alone cannot match.

Two changes make reporting behaviorally sound: replace technical liability terms with language your stakeholders can read without a financial background, and define any performance metric that carries a qualifier like "adjusted." Both decisions reflect how your organization chooses to treat the people it serves.

Organizations that lead on transparency now will hold a competitive advantage as regulators tighten disclosure requirements and investors factor governance quality into their decisions. Those that wait will pay far more to rebuild lost trust than they would have spent maintaining it. This letter asks one thing of your leadership: commission a readability audit of your current financial communications against a plain-language standard and use the results to start revising. The return will show up in stakeholder retention before your next reporting cycle closes.

What Changed and Why

Opening sentence. "I am writing because" gives a busy executive nothing to act on. The opener reads like the writer has not yet figured out what they want to say. The revision leads with the actual problem, stated in terms an executive board would recognize as their own.

Repeated diction. "Create" appears twice in two consecutive clauses, which signals careless writing to anyone reading closely. The revision separates the ethical case and the business case so each gets its own clear statement, then lets the connection between them land.

Mixed metaphor. You cannot improve a blind spot. You reduce it or eliminate it. Using "improve" is a logical error, and in a letter to executive leadership that kind of slip damages credibility faster than it might anywhere else. The revision fixes the language without changing the argument.

Conclusion without stakes. The original ending described steps for implementing change but gave executives no reason to care whether they took them. The revision adds both sides: the advantage of acting now and the cost of waiting until external pressure forces the issue.
