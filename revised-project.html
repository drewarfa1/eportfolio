Revised Project

Open Letter to Company Executives on Financial Transparency  ·  Project 2, Multigenre Persuasive Campaign

The following pages present the first draft and globally revised final draft of the open letter composed for Project 2. The revision addresses four specific failures identified in the first draft: a weak opening sentence, repeated diction in the second paragraph, a mixed metaphor that undermined credibility, and a conclusion that failed to provide executable consequences. Both drafts are presented so that the composing and revision process is visible.

First Draft

To the Board and Executive Leadership,

I am writing because the gap between compliance with accounting regulations and real visibility in business is wider than what has been published by most businesses. Research from the discipline of behavioral economics demonstrates that how financial information is presented impacts the behavior of both a customer and an investor.

The case for visibility has both an ethical and practical basis. Stakeholders deserve accurate representations of financial performance; however, the motivation to provide clearly expressed financial performance reports creates a greater opportunity to create long-term strategic advantages to the business than just the ethical obligation. The Edelman Trust Barometer shows decreases in corporate trust, and businesses that are willing to improve their blind spot with corporate visibility will be rewarded with increased levels of brand loyalty that "manage the earnings" cannot produce.

Reporting in a behaviorally reasonable manner does not necessitate a complete redo of your Finance team; rather, it requires replacing any highly technical language that you currently use to describe liabilities with easily understandable terminology and providing a clear description of what the word 'adjusted' means when used to describe certain performance metrics. This is a matter of how you treat and communicate with the business stakeholders that your company serves.

Revised Draft

To the Board and Executive Leadership,

The gap between regulatory compliance and genuine financial transparency is costing your company stakeholder trust it cannot recover through earnings management alone. Research in behavioral economics demonstrates that how financial information is presented directly shapes the decisions of customers and investors, independent of what the numbers themselves report. That gap is wider than most organizations acknowledge, and the cost of ignoring it is measurable.

The case for transparency operates on both ethical and practical grounds. Stakeholders deserve accurate representations of financial performance. Beyond that obligation, companies that communicate financial results in clear and accessible terms build a structural advantage over competitors who do not. The Edelman Trust Barometer documents a sustained decline in corporate trust across industries. Companies willing to eliminate the blind spots in their financial communication will see measurable increases in brand loyalty that no amount of earnings management can produce.

Achieving behaviorally sound reporting does not require restructuring your Finance team. It requires two targeted changes: replacing technical liability language with terminology your stakeholders can interpret without a financial background, and providing explicit definitions when performance metrics carry qualifiers such as "adjusted." These are decisions about how your organization chooses to treat the people it serves.

The organizations that lead on transparency now will hold a competitive positioning advantage as regulators tighten disclosure requirements and as investors increasingly factor governance quality into allocation decisions. The organizations that do not will find that the cost of rebuilding lost trust is significantly higher than the cost of maintaining it. This letter asks your leadership team to take one concrete step: commission an audit of your current financial communications against a plain-language readability standard and use the results to begin the revision process. The return on that investment will be visible in stakeholder retention before the next reporting cycle closes.

What Changed and Why

Opening sentence. The first draft opened with "I am writing because," which gives an executive reader no reason to continue. The revision opens with the specific problem and frames it immediately in terms of cost, which is the language executive decision-makers respond to.

Repeated diction. The word "create" appeared twice in two consecutive clauses of the first draft, signaling careless revision to any careful reader. The revision separates the ethical and business cases into distinct, connected statements.

Mixed metaphor. "Improve their blind spot" is logically incoherent. Blind spots are eliminated, not improved. The revision replaces this with precise language that preserves the intent without undermining credibility.

Conclusion without stakes. The first draft ended by describing process but not outcome. The revision closes with consequences on both sides of the decision: what executives gain by acting and what they risk by not acting.
